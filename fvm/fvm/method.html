Example Problem

The application codes in this example solve the Euler equations of compressible gas dynamics. In one spatial dimension, the Euler equations are given by

\[\begin{split}\left[ \begin{gathered} \rho \\ \rho u \\ E \end{gathered} \right]_t + \left[ \begin{gathered} \rho u \\ \rho u^2 + p \\ \left(E + p\right)u \end{gathered} \right]_x = 0,\end{split}\]

where \(\rho\) is the density, \(u\) is the velocity, \(E\) is the total engery, and \(p\) is the pressure. This is generally referred to as the conservation form, as it emphasises the property that the Euler equations are mass, momentum, and total energy conserving.

In three dimensions, the conservation form is

\[q_t + f(q)_x + g(q)_y + h(q)_w = 0,\]

where

\[\begin{split}q = \left[ \begin{gathered} \rho \\ \rho u \\ \rho v \\ \rho w \\ E \end{gathered} \right], f(q) = \left[ \begin{gathered} \rho u \\ \rho u^2 + p \\ \rho uv \\ \rho uw \\ \left(E+p\right)u \end{gathered} \right], g(q) = \left[ \begin{gathered} \rho v \\ \rho uv \\ \rho v^2 + p \\ \rho vw \\ \left(E+p\right)v \end{gathered} \right], h(q) = \left[ \begin{gathered} \rho w \\ \rho uw \\ \rho vw \\ \rho w^2 + p \\ \left(E+p\right)w \end{gathered} \right].\end{split}\]

MUSCL Scheme

The MUSCL scheme (Monotonic Upstream-centered Scheme for Conservation Laws) is a second-order (in space) finite volume method that is total variation diminishing (TVD) and can provide accurate simulations of shock dynamics. A succinct, semi-discrete representation of the scheme can be written

\[\frac{du_i}{dt} + \frac{1}{\Delta x_i} \left[ F^{*}_{i+1/2} - F^{*}_{i-1/2} \right],\label{semi}\tag{1}\]

where \(F^{*}_{i\pm 1/2}\) are numerical fluxes that are a non-linear mix of first and second-order slope reconstructions depending on the gradients close to the current cell, and can be written in the form

\[F^{*}_{i\pm 1/2} = f^{low}_{i\pm 1/2} - \phi(r_i) \left( f^{low}_{i\pm 1/2} - f^{high}_{i\pm 1/2} \right)\]

where \(r_i\) is the ratio of successive gradient approximations and \(\phi: r_i \rightarrow [0,1]\) is a limiter function that selects a realistic spatial derivative value. One example of such a limiter function is the minmod limiter

\[\phi_{mm}(r_i) = max[0, min(1,r_i)]; \;\;\lim_{r_i \rightarrow \infty}\phi_{mm}(r_i) = 1.\]

In smooth areas of the flow, \(\phi_{mm}(r_i)\) will be close to one, which will select the higher-order approximation. Near a shock, \(r_i\) will approach infinity and \(\phi_{mm}(r_i)\) will be close to zero and the low-order approximation will be selected.

Hancock Time Evolution

To fully discretize (\(\ref{semi}\)), we can use the Hancock method, which is second-order accurate in time. Hancock is a predictor-corrector method that uses the averages of the reconstructed input quantities to approximate the flux for \(t+\Delta t/2\) in the predictor step. These intermediate quantities are then reconstructed to the faces again, but a Riemann solver is used to compute the corrector step.

Note

The MUSCL-Hancock method documented here is second-order accurate in space and time.

Quantity reconstruction (extrapolation) to faces uses the rule

\[q^n_{i\pm 1/2} = q^n_i \pm \frac{\Delta x_i}{2} \left(\frac{\partial q^n_i}{\partial s}\right),\label{recon}\tag{2}\]

where \(\partial q^n_i/\partial s\) is the flux limited slope.

The predictor step computes intermediate quantities \(q^{*}\) using

\[q^{*}_i = q^n_i - \frac{\Delta t}{2\Delta x_i} \left(q^n_{i-1/2} - q^n_{i+1/2}\right).\]

These quantities are then reconstructed to the faces again by applying (\(\ref{recon}\)). The corrector step is

\[q^{n+1}_i = q^n_i + \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta x} \left( F_{i-1/2} - F_{i+1/2} \right),\]

where \(F()\) is a Riemann solver. The code in this specialization example uses the HLL approximate Riemann sovler.

\[F^{hll} = \frac {S_R F_L - S_L F_R + S_R S_L\left(U_R - U_L\right)} {S_R - S_L}\]

where \(S_T\) and \(S_H\) are the min and max wave speeds taken at the tail and head of the cell interfaces respectively, \(U_L\) and \(U_R\) are the respective intermediate quantities, and \(F_L\) and \(F_R\) are the flux functions evaluated at the respective intermediate quantities.
